Passmark
Twelve years in South Africa's schools
Laura Grant & Alastair Otter

This is a story about South Africa’s schoolchildren. The ones who started school in 2005 when they enrolled in grade one in one of the thousands of government schools around the country. In an ideal world, the majority of these children would have emerged at the end of 2016, after 12 years of schooling, with a national senior certificate (what used to be known as a matric). Unfortunately, this is far from the reality in South Africa's school system.

In January 2005 1.2 million children started grade one in South Africa’s government schools. Each of the circles below represents 2,500 pupils.

In 2005, 1.2 million children started Grade 1, the start of their school careers.
Six years later, in 2011, those children should have been in their last year of primary school, grade seven. But one out of every five children who started in 2005 didn’t make it to grade seven. In 2011, 910,994 pupils were enrolled in grade 7. That's 297,002 fewer pupils in grade seven than started grade one in 2005.

A high number of learners have to repeat grades in South Africa. Research has shown that about one in three children in SA had to repeat a grade at school. Grade repetition gets worse in the higher grades. The grades with the highest percentage of repeaters are grades 9, 10 and 11.

When those kids from the grade one class of 2005 started high school (grade 8) in 2012, their ranks were swollen by an extra 23,500 learners who were most likely repeaters – children who had failed grade eight the previous year and had to repeat it.
