Twelve years in South Africa's schools
Laura Grant & Alastair Otter

This is a story about South Africa’s schoolchildren. The ones who started school in 2005 when they enrolled in grade one in one of the thousands of government schools around the country. In an ideal world, the majority of these children would have emerged at the end of 2016, after 12 years of schooling, with a national senior certificate (what used to be known as a matric). Unfortunately, this is far from the reality in South Africa's school system.

In January 2005 1.2 million children started grade one in South Africa’s government schools. Each of the circles below represents 2,500 pupils.

In 2005, 1.2 million children started Grade 1, the start of their school careers.
Six years later, in 2011, those children should have been in their last year of primary school, grade seven. But one out of every five children who started in 2005 didn’t make it to grade seven. In 2011, 910,994 pupils were enrolled in grade 7. That's 297,002 fewer pupils in grade seven than started grade one in 2005.

When those kids from the grade one class of 2005 started high school (grade 8) in 2012, their ranks were swollen by an extra 23,500 learners who were most likely repeaters – children who had failed grade eight the previous year and had to repeat it.

A high number of learners have to repeat grades in South Africa. Research has shown that about one in three children in SA had to repeat a grade at school. Grade repetition gets worse in the higher grades. The grades with the highest percentage of repeaters are grades 9, 10 and 11.

By the time our 2005 grade ones reached grade 10 there were 1,1-million pupils in their grade. At least another 100,000 repeaters join them grade nine, and an additional 64,000 in grade 10. Research has shown that just over half of the learners in South Africa’s schools had repeated at least one grade by the time they reached grades 10 to 12. One in 10 of them has repeated a grade at least three times.
After grade 10 the numbers drop rapidly. By Grade 12 there were just 665,355 learners, which means that at least half the class of 2005 didn’t make it to grade 12 within 12 years.
And of those who did make it, one in every three will fail their national senior certificate exams.
And only one in every four who wrote the grade 12 exams got a university pass.
Looking at it another way: imagine the 1.2 million pupils that started grade 1 in 2005 are represented by these 100 points.
In 2016, 45 of the original 100 pupils would not yet have reached grade 12. Another 19 would have reached grade 12 but will not pass the year. 36 of those that make it to grade 12 will pass the matric exams, but only 13 of them will achieve a university entrance pass.
Many of the students that don't make it through to grade 12 within 12 years will still reach, and pass, matric a few years later. It is difficult to know exactly how many pupils did make it from grade 1 to grade 12 in 12 years as many of the students in grade 12 in 2016 would have been from previous cohorts.
Passing matric and even getting into university is not a guarantee of success in life but achieving those does improve school leavers' chances of being employed. While failing to reach grade 12, or failing grade 12 increases the likelihood of unemployment.
People who didn’t get their matric certificate are likely to find it the hardest to get a job. Nearly 45% of 15 to 34 year-olds without a matric are unemployed, says StatsSA. 33% of those that did pass matric but don't have a univerisity qualification are unemployed, while the vast majority (87%) of students with a university degree are employed.
